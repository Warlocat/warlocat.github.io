Interaction picture

Interaction picture is a useful tool in quantum mechanics to simplify the time evolution of operators, particularly in the time-dependent perturbation theory. We quickly review this topic here to introduce the Green's function methods.

If the Hamiltonian can be split into two parts, \(H = H_0 + H_I\). The time-independent Hamiltonian \(H_0\) is usually solvable. In the language of quantum chemistry, one can image \(H_0\) as the Hamiltonian of a non-interacting system (or interacting at mean-field level) and \(H_I\) as the Coulomb interaction accounting for electron correlation. Typically, in the discussion of interaction picture, \(H_I\) is time-dependent. But for our purpose, we will first focus on the time-independent case.

Interaction picture and time evolution operator

We define the wave function in the interaction picture as \[ \left| \Psi_I(t) \right\rangle = e^{iH_0t} \left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle \] where \(\left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle\) is the wave function in the Schrödinger picture, i.e., following the time-dependent Schrödinger equation. \[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle = H \left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle \] The time evolution of wave function in interaction picture is thus \[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} \left| \Psi_I(t) \right\rangle = -H_0 e^{iH_0t/\hbar} \left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle + e^{iH_0t/\hbar} H \left| \Psi_S(t) \right\rangle = H_I(t) \left| \Psi_I(t) \right\rangle \] where \[ H_I(t) = e^{iH_0t/\hbar} H_I e^{-iH_0t/\hbar} \] One has to be carefule that \(H_0\) commutes with itself and thus \(e^{iH_0t/\hbar}\), but \(H_I\) and \(H\) do not. And if \([A,B]\neq0\), we will have \(e^{A+B} \neq e^{A}e^{B}\). We can define the operators in the interaction picture as \[ A_I(t) = e^{iH_0t/\hbar} A e^{-iH_0t/\hbar} \] The time evolution of operators in the interaction picture can be shown to be \[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} A_I(t) = [A_I(t), H_0] \] With these, we define the time evolution operator in the interaction picture as \[ |\Psi_I(t)\rangle = U_I(t, t_0) |\Psi_I(t_0)\rangle \] It can easily shown that the time evolution operator in the interaction picture is \[ U_I(t, t_0) = e^{iH_0t_0/\hbar} e^{-iH(t-t_0)/\hbar} e^{-iH_0t_0/\hbar} \] One may recognize that the operator in the middle is the time evolution operator in the Schrödinger picture. It is obvious that the time evolution operator has following properties \begin{align*} &U_I^\dagger(t,t_0) U_I(t,t_0) = I \\ &U_I(t,t) = I \\ &U_I(t_2,t_1) U_I(t_1,t_0) = U_I(t_2,t_0) \end{align*} We can set up a differential equation for the time evolution operator in the interaction picture by \[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} U_I(t,t_0) |\Psi_I(t_0)\rangle = i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\Psi_I(t)\rangle = H_I(t) |\Psi_I(t)\rangle = H_I(t) U_I(t,t_0) |\Psi_I(t_0)\rangle \] which we have included time \(t\) in the interaction Hamiltonian \(H_I(t)\). We have \[ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} U_I(t,t_0) = H_I(t) U_I(t,t_0) \] and \begin{aligned} U_I(t,t_0) & = I - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{t_0}^{t} H_I(t') U_I(t',t_0) dt' \\ & = I + \left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \right) \int_{t_0}^{t} H_I(t') dt' + \left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \right)^2 \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t'} H_I(t') H_I(t'') dt'' dt'+ \cdots \end{aligned} To get the second line, we have iteratively used the first line to substitute the \(U_I(t',t_0)\) in the integral. For the second term, we have \begin{aligned} \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t'} H_I(t') H_I(t'') dt'' dt' &= \frac{1}{2} \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t'} H_I(t') H_I(t'') dt''dt'\\ & + \frac{1}{2}\int_{t_0}^{t}\int_{t''}^{t_0} H_I(t') H_I(t'') dt'dt''\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t'} H_I(t') H_I(t'') dt''dt'\\ & + \frac{1}{2}\int_{t_0}^{t}\int_{t'}^{t_0} H_I(t'') H_I(t') dt''dt'\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t}\left[ \theta(t'-t'')H_I(t')H_I(t'') + \theta(t''-t') H_I(t'') H_I(t') \right] dt''dt'\\ &= \frac{1}{2!} \int_{t_0}^{t} \int_{t_0}^{t}\hat{T} \left\{ H_I(t')H_I(t'') \right\} dt''dt' \end{aligned} where \(\hat{T}\) is the time-ordering operator and \(\theta(t)\) is the Heaviside step function. We have \begin{aligned} U_I(t,t_0) & = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \left( \frac{-i}{\hbar} \right) ^ n \int_{t_0}^t dt_1 \cdots \int_{t_0}^t dt_n \hat{T} \left\{ H_I(t_1)H_I(t_2)\cdots H_I(t_n) \right\} \\ &= \hat{T}\left\{ \exp\left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{t_0}^{t} H_I(t') dt' \right) \right\} \end{aligned}

Adiabatic "switch-on"

The adiabatic "switch-on" is a useful concept in quantum chemistry to relate a time-independent perturbation problem to a time-dependent one. One way to do this is to define the time-dependent perturbation as \[ H_I(t) = \theta(t) H_I \] It means that the perturbation is suddenly "switched on" at time \(t=0\). Instead of switching on the perturbation at \(t=0\), we can choose to switch the perturbation very slowly (adiabatically) from \(t=-\infty\) to \(t=0\). Mathematically, we can define the time-dependent perturbation as \[ H_I(t) = \lim_{\epsilon\rightarrow 0^+} e^{-\epsilon |t|} H_I \] where \(\epsilon\) is a small positive number. This enforce the perturbation to be zero at \(t=\pm\infty\) and gradually increase to \(H_I\) at \(t=0\).

If the system is at some time-indepedent state \(|\Phi_0\rangle\) satisfying \(H_0|\Phi_0\rangle = E_0|\Phi_0\rangle\) at time \(t_0=\pm\infty \), we can define \[ |\Psi_I(0)\rangle = U_{\epsilon,I}(0, -\infty) |\Phi_0\rangle \] which is the time evolution of the state \(|\Phi_0\rangle\) under adiabatically switching on the interaction (differs by a phase factor). It can be shown that the state \(|\Psi_I(0)\rangle\) is the eigenstate of the interacting Hamiltonian \(H = H_0 + H_I\). This is called the Gell-Mann and Low theorem. If the wave function \[ \frac{ U_{\epsilon,I}(0, \pm\infty) |\Phi\rangle}{\langle \Phi_0 |U_{\epsilon,I}(0, \pm\infty) |\Phi\rangle} = \frac{|\Psi_I(0)\rangle}{\langle\Phi_0|\Psi_I(0)\rangle} \] exists, it is the eigenstate of the interacting Hamiltonian \(H = H_0 + H_I\). \[ H \frac{|\Psi_I(0)\rangle}{\langle\Phi_0|\Psi_I(0)\rangle} = E \frac{|\Psi_I(0)\rangle}{\langle\Phi_0|\Psi_I(0)\rangle} \] As a result, we can (in principle) solve the interacting problem by solving the non-interating problem and perform the time evolution under adiabatic "switch-on" using the equation in the last section to evaluate \(U_{\epsilon,I}(0, \pm\infty)\).
